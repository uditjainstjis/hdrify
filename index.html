What Ultra HDR actually is

Ultra HDR is a way of packing a high dynamic range picture inside an ordinary JPEG, so that HDR screens show it glowing far above white while everything else quietly shows the normal photo.

A regular JPEG can only describe brightness up to "white" — code value 255. On a modern display, that white is nowhere near as bright as the panel can go. A MacBook Pro XDR panel, a recent Android flagship or an iPhone can push highlights several times brighter than the white of the page around them. Standard image formats have no way to ask for that.

An Ultra HDR file solves it by carrying two things instead of one:

  • a base image — a completely ordinary SDR JPEG, exactly the picture you started with;
  • a gain map — a second, smaller JPEG in the same file, whose pixels are not colours but multipliers: how much brighter each pixel should render on a display that has the headroom for it.

Two more pieces glue them together: an XMP metadata packet in the hdrgm namespace describing the range the gain map was encoded against, and an MPF (Multi-Picture Format) index telling a decoder where the second image starts inside the file.

The format was published by Google for Android and uses an encoding scheme developed by Adobe; a closely related mechanism is standardised as ISO 21496-1, which is what Apple's platforms adopted. That is why one file can glow on both.

How gain maps work

The gain map stores, per pixel and per channel, the base-2 logarithm of the ratio between the HDR rendition and the SDR one, normalised into the 0–255 range of an 8-bit image. The minimum and maximum of that range travel in the metadata as GainMapMin and GainMapMax. A decoder reverses the normalisation and applies:

hdr = sdr × 2 ^ (log_boost × weight)

The weight is the important part. It is derived from how much headroom the display actually has right now. On a screen with no headroom it lands at zero, the multiplier collapses to 1, and you see the untouched SDR photo. On a screen with plenty of headroom it goes to one and you get the full effect. In between, you get a proportional amount. Nothing clips and nothing breaks — the format degrades smoothly rather than failing.

Why hdrify's default preset changes nothing but brightness

If the gain map is a flat value — the same multiplier everywhere, applied in linear light — then mathematically the operation cannot touch hue, saturation or contrast ratios. It is a scalar on a linear-light triple. That is what the Untouched preset does: the picture is pixel-for-pixel the same photograph, just rendered several times brighter than the white of the page it sits on. The other controls deliberately break that neutrality — a knee spends the boost on highlights only, warmth splits it per channel, bloom spills light off bright regions — but only inside the HDR layer, so the SDR fallback stays the original picture.

How to convert a photo to Ultra HDR

  1. Drop a photo in — JPEG, PNG, WebP, anything your browser can decode. It is read straight from disk into this tab; no file is uploaded anywhere.
  2. Pick a preset. Untouched for a faithful photo that simply glows. Golden for a warm highlight bloom. Neon for a cool, saturated look. Blowout for a deliberately overexposed one.
  3. Adjust the boost. This is the headline number: how many times above SDR white the picture renders. Values around 8–16× read as convincingly bright without looking broken on modest displays.
  4. Check the right-hand preview on an HDR display, with brightness not at maximum. If it looks identical to the left, you have no headroom to see the difference — the file is still correct.
  5. Download. You get a single .jpg. Anything that cannot read gain maps just shows your original photo.

The headroom catch

Even on a perfectly capable device, the effect disappears if the screen is already at maximum brightness — there is no headroom left to boost into. Turn the brightness down a few notches and it appears. Low-power mode and some battery-saver settings also disable HDR rendering entirely. This surprises far more people than the format itself does.

Where the effect survives being uploaded — and where it dies

The gain map lives in a second image plus metadata inside the file. Any service that re-encodes, resizes or strips metadata on upload destroys it. That is most of them, and it is the single biggest source of disappointment with HDR photos.

It reliably dies here

  • LinkedIn profile pictures. Measured: the upload is re-encoded down to 100×100 pixels, roughly 4 KB, a single JPEG segment with every piece of metadata gone. There is nothing left of the gain map.
  • Screenshots. A screenshot captures the rendered SDR pixels, not the file.
  • Any "save as" or edit pass through a tool that does not itself understand gain maps — including most crops, filters and compressors.
  • Services that transcode aggressively to save bandwidth. If a platform shrinks your 4 MB photo to 300 KB, the gain map is not coming with it.

It can survive here

  • Direct file transfer — AirDrop, a cloud-storage link, email attachment, anything that moves the bytes rather than re-encoding them.
  • Photo libraries on Android and Apple platforms, which are gain-map aware.
  • Some feed uploads on some platforms, sometimes. Instagram and Threads have added HDR photo support, but whether it survives depends on the client, the upload path and whether you touched the in-app editor — applying a filter or cropping in the composer generally re-renders to SDR. Test it before you rely on it.

The honest summary: treat the glow as a bonus that appears on direct shares and modern galleries, not as something you can guarantee on any given social platform. Because the fallback is your untouched original, there is no downside to trying.

Privacy: nothing is uploaded

hdrify is a static page. The conversion runs in your browser using Canvas and a vendored, MIT-licensed copy of libultrahdr — there is no server, no account, no queue, no watermark, and no upload step to opt out of. You can verify it: open your browser's network tab, convert a photo, and watch that no request leaves. The page loads no third-party scripts, fonts or analytics either.

A practical consequence: large images are limited by your own device's memory and CPU, not by a plan tier. A very large photo on a phone may take a few seconds.

Frequently asked questions

Is hdrify free?

Yes — completely free, with no account, no export limit, no resolution cap and no watermark. It is an open-source static page; the conversion happens on your own device, so there is nothing for anyone to charge for.

Does my photo get uploaded to a server?

No. The encoder runs entirely in your browser tab. There is no backend. Your image never leaves your device, which you can confirm in your browser's network inspector.

Why does my converted photo look exactly the same?

Almost always one of three reasons: your display is not HDR-capable; your screen brightness is already at maximum, so there is no headroom left to boost into; or you are viewing it in software that ignores gain maps, such as Firefox at the time of writing. Turn the brightness down a few notches and view it in Chrome or Safari on an HDR display.

Will an Ultra HDR file look wrong on old phones or SDR monitors?

No. Decoders that do not understand the gain map simply display the base JPEG, which is your original photo. That backwards compatibility is the entire point of the format, so the file is always safe to send to anyone.

Does the glow survive uploading to LinkedIn, Instagram or WhatsApp?

LinkedIn profile pictures destroy it — we measured the upload being re-encoded to 100×100 pixels at roughly 4 KB with all metadata stripped. Most services that re-encode uploads strip gain maps the same way. Instagram and Threads have added HDR photo support, but whether a given upload keeps it depends on the client and upload path, and using the in-app editor generally re-renders to SDR. Direct transfers such as AirDrop, cloud links and email attachments preserve the file intact.

Does hdrify change the colours of my photo?

Not on the default Untouched preset. It applies a flat multiplier in linear light, which is mathematically incapable of altering hue, saturation or contrast ratios — the picture is identical, just rendered brighter. The warmth, vividness, knee and bloom controls do change the look, but only inside the HDR layer; the SDR fallback stays your original photo either way.

What is the difference between Ultra HDR and an HDR photo from my camera?

A camera HDR mode usually merges several exposures into one well-exposed SDR image. Ultra HDR is about display: it stores extra brightness information so a capable screen can render highlights above SDR white. hdrify does not invent detail your photo never had — it maps the photo you have into your display's headroom.

What file formats can I put in, and what comes out?

Anything your browser can decode as an image goes in — JPEG, PNG, WebP, and usually HEIC on Apple platforms. What comes out is always a single .jpg: an Ultra HDR JPEG containing your SDR base image, the gain map, XMP metadata and an MPF index.

Can I convert video too?

Not in the browser. The companion command-line tool, hdrify.py in the GitHub repository, handles video by encoding HEVC 10-bit PQ (smpte2084).
